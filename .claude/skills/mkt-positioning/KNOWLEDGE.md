## April Dunford — Obviously Awesome (the spine of this skill)

> Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at
> something a defined market cares a lot about.

### The 5 components

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What would customers do if you didn't
   exist? Often "nothing" or "a spreadsheet" — not a named competitor.
   For tax-contester, the alternatives are:
   - Do nothing (pay the assessed bill)
   - Self-appeal cold (walk in with no evidence)
   - Hire a 30–50%-of-savings firm
   - Try to DIY with Zillow comps
2. **Unique attributes.** Features only you have. (Not benefits — those
   come next.)
3. **Value.** The benefit those attributes deliver. Translate features →
   outcomes. *"4-pass weighted scoring"* → *"comps that actually match
   your house, not the foreclosure two streets over."*
4. **Customers who care a lot.** The segment for whom that value matters
   most. Sharpen by disqualifying — who is NOT a fit?
5. **Market category.** The frame of reference that makes your value
   obvious. The same product positioned as "AI tax software" vs
   "evidence packet for homeowners" attracts different audiences and
   sets different price expectations.

### Two tests

- **The completion test:** "Compared to ___, we are the only one that ___."
  If you can't fill both blanks, positioning is not done.
- **The category test:** Remove the category label from your homepage.
  Can a first-time visitor still tell what this is? If no, the category
  is doing too much work.

## Jobs-to-be-Done

Customers "hire" a product to make progress on a job.

For tax-contester the job is roughly:
> *"When my assessment notice arrives and feels wrong, help me push back
> without paying a firm or looking foolish in front of the Board."*

Three dimensions to address:

- **Functional:** get evidence, file on time, win the protest.
- **Emotional:** feel like a competent adult, not a sucker.
- **Social:** be the neighbor who knew what to do.

Skip any dimension and the messaging feels off — usually too "feature-
list" (functional only) or too "lifestyle" (emotional only).

## Messaging hierarchy

```
Value Prop  (one sentence: what + for whom + uniquely why)
├── Pillar 1  (a reason to believe)  → 2–3 proof points
├── Pillar 2                         → 2–3 proof points
└── Pillar 3                         → 2–3 proof points
```

Three pillars is the right number. Two is thin, four is unmemorable.
Each pillar must be backed by something specific — a number, a
screenshot, a citation, a quote. A pillar without proof is a slogan.

## Anti-positioning (the underused move)

Decide what you will NOT say. Concrete examples for tax-contester:

- Won't say "AI-powered" — commodity, not a differentiator here.
- Won't say "guaranteed savings" — legally risky, also dishonest.
- Won't say "disrupting" — Midwest homeowners hate that word.
- Won't promise a win rate without published data.

Anti-positioning works because every "no" sharpens the remaining "yes".

## Category choice matters more than people think

The same product can sit on multiple shelves:

| Category framing | Audience implied | Price expectation |
|---|---|---|
| "AI property tax software" | Investors, multi-property owners | $$$, SaaS |
| "Evidence packet for homeowners" | DIY homeowners | Free–$ |
| "Property tax appeal service" | Anyone, with handoff to a pro | $$, contingency |
| "Civic transparency tool" | Activist / journalist | Free |

Pick the frame where your unique attributes look strongest *and* the
audience actually exists in volume. For tax-contester v1 — DIY
homeowners, free, Polk/Dallas — the right shelf is **"evidence packet
for homeowners"**. The data + packet ARE the product; positioning as
"software" undersells both.

## The 10-second rule (hero test)

If a first-time visitor can't answer all three in 10 seconds, the
positioning has failed:

1. What is this?
2. Who is it for?
3. Why should I care?

Apply this test to every artifact downstream of positioning — hero copy,
ad copy, SEO meta description, email subject lines.

## Local credibility as a positioning lever

For a hyper-local tool, "built by an Iowan, for Polk + Dallas" is itself
a differentiator. National competitors literally cannot say this. Lean
into:

- Named counties, cities, streets
- Iowa Code citation (§441.37)
- Board of Review by name
- Seasonal calendar (April 30 deadline)

This is positioning, not just SEO. It tells the visitor "this person
knows my situation specifically."
